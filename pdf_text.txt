Empire of Gatan: The Burnt One Returns

“Fair indeed are its pastures, gentle its hills, deep and ancient its forests, and awe-inspiring
its mountains. Its people are counted amongst the most civilised, learned, and honourable in
all the lands. Wise are its laws, ordained by the Celestial Emperor himself. Expansive and
well-planned are its cities, mighty and magnificent its fortifications. Rich in treasure and
steeped in culture, Gatan holds within its borders noble Knights, cunning Wizards, stout and
steadfast peasants, gracious Lords, and Ladies of surpassing beauty. Though it be not
Heaven, it is the closest mortal realm may aspire to reach. Let me, Richjo the Sage,
introduce you to noble Gatan, an Empire to challenge the glories of ages past!”

-- Richjo the Sage, A New Traveler’s Guide to the Empire, Sotan University Press, 49 After Unity.

The Protagonists of this Tale:

Ambrosius, a weaver of spells and seeker of hidden truths.
Bors, a stalwart knight of unyielding heart.
Evelyn Greenvale, swift of bow and keen of eye.
Ivan Ironheart, a warrior bold, whose blade knows no fear.

These four, bound by fate, have wandered the roads together for nigh on two winters, their
strengths woven as threads in a tapestry of trust.

Chapter One: A Road Less Trodden (Early Autumn, 50 AU)
The Journey’s Dawn
In the waning days of summer, when the leaves of the trees began to gleam with the first
gold of autumn, there came unto Ossoway—a humble town upon the wild outskirts of the
Empire of Gatan—a band of wanderers bold and true. Ambrosius, sage of subtle craft; Bors,
knight of unyielding honor; Evelyn Greenvale, keen-eyed huntress of the wilds; and Ivan
Ironheart, warrior of fierce blade and fearless heart—these four had trodden many a perilous
path together. In Ossoway they had wrought deeds of valor as sellswords, their names
whispered with reverence among the folk of that frontier hold. Into this quiet haven stepped
Deean, an Imperial Wizard clad in robes of twilight grey, woven as though from the mists of
dawn, her eyes agleam with arcane wisdom. With her was young Dargon, a lad of fiery spirit
and noble lineage, her charge and companion. She offered unto the company a task of
weight and worth: to guard her and Dargon upon a journey to White Dragon Fort in Two Fort
County, the seat of Count Zoltran, Dargon’s sire. The path they must tread wound through
Snakebite Pass, a narrow cleft hewn amidst the rugged Jank Hills, far from the empire’s
smooth and tended roads. With hearts resolute and oaths unspoken, the fellowship accepted
her charge, and at the breaking of the next dawn they set forth. Deean and Dargon rode in
an open wain, its boards laden with provisions ample for the road ahead, while the company
walked as their shield and sword.
Dargon, a youth fair of face and eager of soul, burned with a boundless zeal for the journey,
his heart alight with dreams of adventure and the long-awaited meeting with his father. Count
Zoltran, eighteen winters past, had departed the imperial city of Sotan, tasked by Emperor
Ilmar the First—a ruler of stern vision—to secure his family’s eastern demesne, a wild land
bordering the empire’s edge. Ilmar sought to restore the realm to its ancient glory, lost amid
the ruinous Ducal War and the fell invasion of the Burning Heart Horde from the east.
Dargon, trained in the courts of Sotan by masters of war and wisdom, had now come of age,
his spirit eager to join his father in bringing light to the barbarous folk of the east and smiting
any remnant of the Horde that dared linger. A lad of high cheer he was, so long as none
spoke ill of the empire or the Celestial Emperor Maximus, whose divine will upheld the
realm. With wide eyes and a ready ear, he drank deep of the company’s tales, their words
kindling his youthful fire.

The Snare That Was None
At the threshold of Snakebite Pass, where the hills rose stark and foreboding, the company
beheld a fallen tree barring their way, its trunk hewn and laid with a purpose that whispered
of ambush. Their hands tightened upon their weapons, yet no foe emerged from the
shadows. Instead, Jiolo, a hunter of those wild lands, stepped forth, her voice steady as she
spoke. She told them of goblins who had felled the tree with crude axes and warned that the
goblinoid kind had grown bold and numerous of late, their presence a shadow creeping ever
nearer. With a nod of parting, Jiolo took her leave, vanishing into the hills, though fate would
bring her again into their path at the Halfway House, the last refuge ere they reached White
Dragon Fort.

The Halfway House
This flimsy inn stood as a bastion of warmth amid the wilds, its hearth tended by Henra, a
jolly master whose laughter rang like a bell through the rafters. Within its walls lingered only
two others: Smella and Gartu, warriors of weathered steel and stern countenance. From
their lips, and from Jiolo’s once more, came tidings grim: a troll had been spied lurking in the
woods nigh the pass, its shadow a terror to the folk, and a goblin warband swelled in the
hills, their numbers foretelling strife. Deean, her gaze sharp with thought, weighed the
wisdom of seeking further aid, yet her heart recoiled from the dwellers of the Halfway House.
Many in these lands yet clung in secret to the worship of the Earth Mother, a faith once
sovereign across the known world, until the Doctrine of Thenos cast it down, banning all
creeds save that of the Celestial Emperor. Thus, mistrust stayed her hand, and the company
pressed on alone.

The Mausoleum of Hilltop
At the rising of the sun, the fellowship departed the inn and came soon unto the Mausoleum
of Hilltop, its ancient stones cloaked in a thick mist that hung heavy as a shroud. Here, they
knew, had been fought the Battle of Hilltop in the dark days of the Ducal War, when Emperor
Sotan’s death had plunged the realm into chaos. Four dukes, proud and fell, had gathered
with forty thousand souls beneath their banners, and there clashed in a storm of steel and
blood so dire that all four lords fell, and the greater part of their host with them. The empire
bled from that wound, its strength broken, and soon after came the Burning Heart Horde, a
tide of fire and ruin from the east. Now, the dead stirred within the mausoleum, their rest
defiled, and a host of restless spirits barred the pass ahead. Deep beneath the earth, in a
tomb cold and silent, the shade of Duke Ilos himself arose, his voice a whisper of wrath and
sorrow. He spoke of goblin hands that had desecrated their graves, plundering gold and
relics hallowed by time. Only with an oath of vengeance did he grant them passage: the
goblinoids must perish, the stolen treasures returned, that the fallen might sleep once more.
With solemn words, the company swore to this charge and pressed onward through the
veiled pass.

The Troll Bridge
Ahead, rising from the mists, stood a stone bridge, its arches a sturdy testament to the craft
of the Old Empire, whose records had faded into shadow, leaving only such works to speak

of its splendor. Though the new empire labored to reclaim that lost glory, the bridge stood as
a silent reminder of the heights yet unscaled. As they drew nigh, a rumbling snore echoed
from below, and Evelyn, silent as a wraith upon the wind, scouted the way. Beneath the
span, at the edge of a sheer cliff, she spied a troll, its bulk sprawled in slumber. The
company sought to fall upon it unawares, yet the beast’s ears twitched at their approach,
and with startling swiftness it awoke and clambered atop the bridge, its roar shaking the
stones. Dargon, aflame with righteous zeal, seized the moment to purge this abomination
from the land, charging forth with Bors and Ivan at his heels. The troll swung a great club, its
blow hurling Dargon meters hence, tumbling o’er the earth, yet by some grace—likely
Deean’s subtle art—he rose with naught but bruises to mark the strike. Ivan followed, his
greatsword flashing like lightning, cleaving the troll’s hide so deep that it staggered and fell.
Bors, with a swift and final stroke, struck off its head, and Ivan raised the grim trophy upon a
pike, a stern warning to all who defied the empire’s might—a deed that brought a fierce grin
to Dargon’s lips.

The Goblin Caves
Ere long, the company came upon a barrier of logs, their ends hammered deep into the
earth, blocking the road utterly. To the left rose a cliff, nigh thirty meters high, its face too
sheer to scale with ease, while to the right a gentler hill offered passage afoot. Amid the
timbers they found a wooden portal, and with strength of arm, Ivan and Bors forced it
asunder, its groan a thunderous herald that echoed through the pass. Within lay a cavern old
and forsaken, a labyrinth carved beneath the hill, its depths cloaked in pitch-black shadow.
Scarcely had they crossed the threshold when goblins assailed them—some loosing arrows
from the dark, others lurking in a small chamber nigh the entrance. Bors charged down the
tunnel, his shield a bulwark against the storm of shafts, while Ivan stormed the chamber, his
blade reaping swift death upon the three goblins within. Ambrosius wove a spell of light, its
radiance piercing the gloom, though the foe retreated deeper into shadow. From that
darkness came a lucky dart, striking Bors in the neck with grievous force. He staggered, his
breath faltering, yet with a mighty effort he rose once more, fighting on through pain and
weariness. Arrows sang through the air, Evelyn’s bow answering in kind, while Ambrosius
mended their wounds and lit their path with ceaseless care.
The tide seemed to turn in their favor until Ivan cried out, a stab of unseen sorcery piercing
his vitals. Ambrosius’s light revealed a goblin mage, its hands twisting a corn doll in a rite of
dark power, wounding Ivan from afar. Sensing his time was short, Ivan summoned his last
strength and charged, his blade hewing the fiend asunder like a child’s toy. The remaining
goblins broke and fled, yet one slipped away into the dark, its fate unknown. Ambrosius
tended the company’s hurts, his skill mending Bors’s grievous neck wound with a surgeon’s
precision. In their search of the caverns, they found Sir Lodas, a knight of the Order of
Thrum, bound in chains within a cell. He spoke of his capture three days past, taken while
journeying to White Dragon Fort, and with gratitude he joined their cause. Bors, exploring a
chamber bypassed in the fray, discovered a pool at its heart, its waters glinting with gold
nuggets and cradling a dead goblin clutching a great green gem. As he reached for the
corpse, the waters stirred and rose into a humanoid form, a water elemental fierce and
vigilant, guarding its trove. Ambrosius, wise in such matters, deemed the gold an offering
and counselled retreat, for the creature’s wrath was not to be trifled with. Deean, her voice
firm, urged them to hasten hence, Dargon’s safety paramount, and so they cleared the logsfrom the road and departed, noting as they went a second entrance hidden amid tangled
bushes.

The White Dragon Fort: The Arrival
The fort lay nigh, yet the company, wearied by their trials, sought rest ere pressing on. In a
clearing near the road, hidden from prying eyes, they made camp. In the deep of night, after
his watch, Bors found Sir Lodas vanished, his absence a riddle cloaked in shadow. Troubled
but undeterred, they resumed their march at dawn. Emerging from Snakebite Pass, they
beheld a broad valley, its meadows fair and its fields tilled with care, stretching afore them.
Upon a low hill in the distance rose White Dragon Fort, its sturdy walls a sentinel over the
land, flanked by a canyon where the river Nyx flowed with quiet might. The road grew
smooth and well-kept, dotted with houses where folk labored in the soil.
The fort itself, though not vast, was wrought for defense, its walls soaring more than ten
meters high and two meters thick, gleaming in impeccable order. Carven upon them were
delicate reliefs, scenes that seemed to depict a coronation of old. Guards stood vigilant at
the gate and atop the ramparts, their eyes sharp. Deean stepped forth, her voice ringing
clear as she proclaimed the coming of the count’s son. One guard hastened within, while the
others bade the company enter with courteous welcome. Ere long, a tall figure emerged, his
hair grey as winter frost, his armor bearing the imperial emblem—Count Zoltran, striding
through a gathering throng. Father and son gazed upon one another for a fleeting moment,
then Dargon rushed forward, and they embraced, tears welling in their eyes as Zoltran
whispered words lost to the wind. They clung thus for a time, bound by blood and years
apart.
Beside the count stood Cowena, High Priestess of the fort, a plump woman with braided hair
and a smile broad as the dawn, clad in the robes of the Imperial Cult. She joined Deean at
once, their voices mingling in earnest discourse. After a brief span, Zoltran raised Dargon’s
hand aloft, presenting him to the gathered folk, who erupted in cheers and jubilation, their
voices a tide of joy. Then the count turned to Ambrosius, Bors, Evelyn, and Ivan, offering
thanks to each in turn with grave courtesy. He summoned Cowena and Deean to his side,
bidding them to ensure the company’s comfort and inviting them as honored guests to the
night’s festivities. A place of rest was granted to them, and Gerrick, a veteran soldier of
grizzled years, guided them through the fort’s halls. At the temple, Cowena bestowed upon
them vials of healing draughts and a purse of coin, payment for their perilous service.
Thereafter, the company scattered to explore—some to the library’s ancient tomes, others to
the tavern’s cheer or the shops’ wares.

The White Dragon Fort: The First Night
The celebration that night was modest in scale yet grand in spirit, a marvel for so remote a
hold. The tables groaned with fare of every kind, and a bard’s voice soared, weaving tales in
song. The folk made merry, and the company met many a soul: Miller, captain of the guard,
stern and stalwart; Martin Longbow, the fort’s chief scout, swift and silent; and sundry
shopkeepers of honest trade. Bors, seizing the moment, presented to the count a title of
land, earned through years of service in the Imperial Army, its parchment signed by GeneralKael Stormblade himself, granting him a demesne nigh the fort. Zoltran, with a nod of
solemn promise, vowed to honor it swiftly.
All seemed well until a foul stench crept into the hall, like rotten cabbages or rusted iron,
growing ever stronger. Then the earth trembled beneath their feet, a low rumble that stirred
the heart to dread. The revelers leapt up, rushing beyond the walls to behold the cause,
some crying out as the quaking grew fierce. Outside, they saw a blinding light, a spear of
radiance joining earth and sky, its root striking where Black Dragon Fort stood—the twin hold
across the river Nyx, now claimed by the Burning Heart Horde. Though distant it seemed, its
nearness chilled their blood, an omen dark and dire.

The White Dragon Fort: The Next Weeks
The shadow of that night lingered, a weight upon the fort’s folk come morn. No further sign
came, yet unease gnawed at all, for tidings from Black Dragon Fort were ever ill. The
company wove themselves into the fort’s life, joining patrols along its bounds, learning the
craft of its defenses, and hearing tales of recent assaults. Zoltran was told of Sir Lodas’s
vanishing, and with the count’s wisdom and the testimony of others, they deemed him an
impostor, his visage and tale unlike the true knights of Thrum known to the fort.
Other deeds marked those days, weaving a tapestry of toil and trial within the walls of White
Dragon Fort. Ambrosius, sage of subtle craft, lingered oft within the temple’s library, its
ancient shelves laden with tomes of forgotten lore. There he delved deep into the mysteries
of the arcane, guided by Deean, whose wisdom flowed like a wellspring, clear and
boundless, feeding his eager mind with secrets of power and light. Long hours they spent in
discourse, the flickering candles casting shadows upon parchments old, as Ambrosius
sought to plumb the depths of knowledge that she, an Imperial Wizard, held in her keeping.
Evelyn Greenvale, huntress of the wilds, roamed far afield with Martin Longbow, the fort’s
chief scout, whose tread was silent as the wind upon the grass. Together they mastered the
art of reading the land about the fort, her senses sharpened to its every whisper—the rustle
of leaves, the faint track of beast or foe, the murmur of streams beneath the earth. Through
wooded vale and windswept hill she honed her craft, her eyes keen as the hawk’s, learning
the secrets of the wilds from one whose life had been spent in their embrace.
Bors and Ivan, warriors of iron will, bent their days to the clangor of steel, training beneath
the stern eye of Captain Miller, a soldier seasoned by countless campaigns. Their blades
met in a martial dance, ringing with the song of war, as Miller’s weathered hand honed their
skill to a razor’s edge. Each clash of sword and shield was a lesson wrought in sweat and
strength, their prowess growing ever sharper under his tutelage, a testament to the fort’s
unyielding resolve.
Dargon, son of Zoltran, ventured forth on perilous missions beside Miller and Martin, his
youthful spirit tempered anew in the crucible of battle’s fire. Oft he returned bearing the
marks of strife—wounds etched upon his flesh, his frame weary yet unbowed, his
once-boundless zeal now forged into a steady determination. Through trial and blood he was
sculpted, emerging as a leader worthy of men’s fealty, his heart resolute, his eyes fixed upon
a horizon of duty and honor, a transformation borne of the wilds and the blade.
Yet shadowed tidings came to mar their labors: word reached the fort that the pass through
Snakebite Pass remained sealed by the restless dead of Hilltop, their spectral wrath barring
the flow of supplies so direly needed. The wagons faltered, unable to breach the veil of
spirits, leaving the fort’s stores to dwindle, a silent threat amid their vigilance.
And in those days, Deean departed northward on an errand cloaked in urgency, her purpose
veiled from all save her own counsel. Pressing was her need, and she left the company to
their tasks, her absence a quiet void amidst the fort’s ceaseless watch, her path bent toward
mysteries yet unrevealed.

The Impostor
Ere long, reports of goblinoid stirrings roused the fort, and the company, now trusted for their
prowess, was sent to seek the truth. After hours amid the woods, they came upon ruins old
and crumbling, where a troll loomed in the distance. The battle that ensued was fierce and
swift. Evelyn, with an arrow loosed from her bow, felled the beast with a single shot to its
brow, a marvel of marksmanship. Goblins charged from the shadows, yet Bors and Ivan
dispatched them with ease, their blades a whirlwind of death. All seemed in hand until the
troll rose anew, its wounds knit by dark craft, and a shaman emerged with more goblins at its
call. Then strode forth the false Sir Lodas, his treachery laid bare as he struck at the
company. Swiftly they slew the shaman, ending its fell rites, and Ivan clove the troll once
more, its body burned to ash by Ambrosius’s torch. Bors met the impostor blade to blade,
their shields ringing with each clash, until at last the traitor fell, bound in chains and led back
to White Dragon Fort, a prisoner of their valor.

Chapter Two: The Lost Outpost (late Autumn, 50 AU)
Of the Promised Land
Within the sturdy walls of White Dragon Fort, Priestess Cowena, robed in the sacred
vestments of the Imperial Cult, received the impostor knight, bound in chains from the
company’s last perilous venture. With solemn voice, she decreed that he be cast into the
dungeons below, there to await the coming of an Imperial Inquisitor, an interrogator skilled in
the unraveling of shadowed minds. Yet hope for swift justice was dim, for the pass through
the Jank Hills remained sealed at the Mausoleum of Hilltop, where restless spirits held sway.
Several supply convoys, guarded by stalwart Knights of Thrum, had been turned back,
denied passage by the wrathful dead, their wails a barrier none could breach.
In this hour of need, Cowena summoned before the company one Granny Teir, a veteran of
grizzled years and weathered courage, whose tale was steeped in the blood and fire of days
long past. More than three-and-twenty winters ago, Teir had stood among the defenders of
Garrison House, an outpost raised as a spearhead of the empire’s ambition to stretch its
borders eastward. The wild lands about it were home to barbarian tribes, chief among them
the Bear People, who looked upon the outpost with wary eyes, deeming it an intrusion upon
their ancient dominion. Yet Captain Nerran, the commander of that time, a man of honor and
guile, had woven peace through trade and fair words. So great was his worth that the Bear

People—or Pelwe, as some named them—bestowed upon him the Spirit of the Bear, a relic
of their tribe, an axe of mighty craft given only to a champion proven in valor.
But peace, like spring frost, melted under the heat of treachery. In time, a shadow fell upon
the Bear People, for the Burning Heart Horde, with their dark magics and honeyed lies,
corrupted a portion of the tribe. These turncoats, joined by a towering giant of the wilds,
stormed Garrison House in a tempest of ruin. The giant’s fists sundered the entrance, and
the garrison fell swiftly, overwhelmed by numbers and sorcery. Captain Nerran fought with
the fury of a storm, the Spirit of the Bear felling many a foe ere he too succumbed to the tide
of death. Teir, struck down in the fray, awoke amid the carnage, dazed and wracked with
pain, to find herself the sole survivor. As the barbarians gathered their fallen, she slipped
away through a breach in the western wall, her heart heavy with loss.
Swiftly she bore word to White Dragon Fort, yet no aid could be spared, for the fort’s
strength was bent wholly to its own defense against the Horde’s ceaseless wrath. When
Emperor Ilmar the First arose and drove the invaders back, Teir was sent to the capital of
Sotan, lauded for her honorable deeds. Yet the memory of Garrison House lingered as a
wound unhealed, a burning shame that she had not avenged her fallen kin. Six moons past,
fate turned her path once more, and she returned to White Dragon Fort as a
Sergeant-Scout, seasoned and resolute, seeking a chance to reclaim the honor lost in that
dark hour.
And so it was that fate smiled upon her, albeit with a grim visage. Three nobles of the
empire, seeking sport in the wilds, had ventured to Garrison House to camp while hunting
great beasts, their trophies destined for the halls of Sotan. Days ago they vanished, and
Cowena, in communion with the Celestial Emperor Maximus, sought divine counsel. The
signs were fell—visions of blood and cries of torment—and thus she charged Teir to seek the
truth, calling upon Ambrosius, Bors, Evelyn, and Ivan to lend their strength. The outpost, it
was noted, stood nigh the land granted to Bors for his service to the empire, a demesne
revealed to Cowena by the fort’s Head-Scout. She deemed it fitting that Bors should not
miss the chance to behold his inheritance, and so the company rode forth, Teir at their head.

The Garrison House
After half a day’s ride through the crisp air of late autumn, when the leaves lay thick and
golden upon the earth, they crested a hill, and Teir bade them halt. Before them, half a
league distant upon another rise, stood Garrison House, its broken walls a silhouette against
the sky. A dirt road wound down into the small valley betwixt the hills—a valley that was
Bors’s own land—and thence up to the outpost’s ruined gate. As they drew nigh, the rubble
of the shattered entrance loomed stark, a testament to the giant’s wrath of old. Just beyond,
a grim sight met their eyes: a cross stood planted in the earth, and upon it hung a man, his
fine garments stained with dried blood, his life long fled. Here was Mozo, the first of the lost
hunters.
Bors, moved by duty, stepped forth to free the body from its cruel perch, yet as his hands
neared the corpse, a spirit bound within it lashed out, its malice a cold wind against his soul.
With steadfast will, he resisted the assault and drew back, unharmed but wary. Ambrosius,wise in the ways of the unseen, pondered the dark craft that tethered the shade and wove a
counter-spell, its words a soft radiance that set the spirit free, granting it rest at last.
Though safe from the wraith, the company heard stirrings from the outpost’s broken maw, a
murmur of life within. Teir, her memory sharp as steel, led them westward to the breach
through which she had escaped those three-and-twenty winters past. As they approached,
Evelyn’s keen eyes spied a figure perched in a tree nigh the opening—Teagan, the second
hunter, hidden there in desperate vigil, seeking a way to free her companions. She
descended and spoke swiftly, her voice edged with fear: the hunters had been set upon by
barbarians, aided by a man clad in black robes who wielded the power to raise the dead.
Strife had broken their alliance, and the necromancer had barred himself within the outpost’s
great tower.
The company pressed through thick bushes and slipped into the stronghold’s heart. There
they beheld a small shrine at its center, flanked by crumbling barracks to the left and a stable
to the right. Far to the left, beside the barracks, rose a round tower, its stones scorched yet
standing. South of the shrine paced half a dozen skeleton warriors, their bones rattling in
mechanical rhythm, heedless of the intruders. From the stable emerged the barbarians, their
stance wary but not yet hostile. Ivan, his blood hot with the empire’s zeal, yearned to purge
the outpost of these wild folk—and perchance Teir with them, for her origins stirred his
mistrust—but the company, in wisdom, chose parley over steel.
Their leader, Pleaga the Red, spoke of Gar the Traveller, a man who had come among the
Bear People with tidings of the Spirit of the Bear’s resting place. Roused by this promise,
they had formed a warband to reclaim their relic. At Garrison House they found the hunters,
who resisted their claim, and battle ensued. Mozo fell in the fray, Teagan fled to the trees,
and Morgan, the third, was taken and bound within the stable. Pleaga recounted how Gar’s
demeanor turned strange and fell: he demanded Mozo’s corpse be bound to a cross,
weaving defiling magics upon it, and summoned the skeletons to guard the outpost, barring
all passage. When Pleaga challenged him, he shunned her words and locked himself within
the tower. The tale weighed heavy upon the company, and after counsel, they forged an
alliance with the Bear People to storm the tower and bring Gar to heel.

The Outpost’s Tower
The tower’s door stood resolute, unyielding to force of arm. Yet Ambrosius, deft with subtler
arts, picked the lock with nimble fingers, and the portal swung wide. Within lay a great well at
the tower’s heart, while stairs spiraled upward along the walls. The stones bore scars of fire,
a legacy of battles past, yet the structure held firm, though the upper floors and walls gaped
with holes, offering glimpses of the levels above. Bors, his eyes sharp, spied movement high
within and hastened to the stairs, only to be struck by a bolt of dark sorcery from the
necromancer above. The pain seared his flesh, yet he steadied himself, and Ambrosius, with
healing hands, restored his strength, urging him onward.
Then chaos descended like a storm unleashed. From the well clambered zombies, with
strangely preserved forms, a mockery of life, assailing the company with claw and moan.
From behind came the skeletons, their bones clattering as they struck. Teir froze, her breath
caught in her throat, for among the undead stood her comrades of old—friends lost, and

Captain Nerran himself, their faces nigh unchanged by the passage of three-and-twenty
winters. Shock drove her back, her heart torn by memory, yet duty roused her to fight. A
mighty battle erupted: Ivan charged Nerran, his blade hungry for retribution; the Bear People
met the zombies with fierce cries; Teir guarded their flank, her spear a wall against the
skeletons; Evelyn loosed arrows with deadly grace; and Ambrosius followed Bors in pursuit
of the necromancer, their steps swift upon the stair.
Bors and Ambrosius hunted the wizard to the second floor, yet Gar, cunning and desperate,
slipped through a breach in the wall, clambering down to the earth below and fleeing into the
forest’s embrace. The chase was grueling, the air thick with autumn’s chill. Ambrosius
faltered, pausing to catch his breath, but Bors pressed on, his will unyielding. At last he
overtook Gar, who turned with a feeble spell to strike. Bors tripped him with ease, then
smote him with his shield, felling him senseless to the ground.
Meanwhile, below, Pleaga clove through the zombie captain Nerran with a warrior’s might,
and Teir, Evelyn, Ivan, and the Bear People gained the upper hand against the undead host.
Yet suddenly, as if a thread were cut, every risen corpse collapsed, their dark animation
snuffed out with Gar’s defeat, and silence fell upon the tower.

Aftermath
Ivan, his heart set upon claiming the Spirit of the Bear, had sought Nerran’s death for its
prize, yet when Pleaga struck the final blow, he honorably yielded her right to the great axe,
the relic of her people. Pleaga, proud and fierce, deemed no leave of his was needed, but
the matter rested there, unspoken further.
Bors and Ambrosius returned with their quarry—Gar the Traveller, bound tight and stripped
of menace, a shadow of the dread he once wielded. The hunters were reclaimed, though
Mozo lay dead beyond recall, Teagan shaken but alive, and Morgan freed from his bonds in
the stable. The Bear People, their purpose fulfilled, praised the company’s aid and took their
leave, satisfied with the day’s work. Ambrosius sought to kindle new bonds, inviting Pleaga
and her kin to White Dragon Fort for parley. She vowed to weigh their words and departed
with her warband into the wilds.
Thereafter, the company turned their steps back to White Dragon Fort, its sturdy walls a
welcome sight. They delivered their captive unto the keeping of the garrison, and he was
borne swiftly to the shadowed depths of the dungeons below. That night, Bors prepared a
humble feast for the company, his hands deft over the fire, and bade Teir join them. A spark
of warmth grew between them, his interest plain, and she, with quiet gladness, welcomed it.
